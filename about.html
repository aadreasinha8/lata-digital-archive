I

About the Project

Chitralekha: Lata Mangeshkar Digital Archive is an open-access digital humanities project devoted to the systematic documentation, contextualisation, and scholarly analysis of the recorded repertoire of Lata Mangeshkar (28 September 1929 – 6 February 2022). Widely recognised as the most recorded artist in the history of music, Lata Mangeshkar's voice shaped the sonic and emotional fabric of Indian cultural life across more than seven decades, encompassing tens of thousands of recordings in at least thirty-six languages.

The archive takes its name from the Sanskrit chitralekha — literally "one who paints pictures in words and sound" — a term that captures the way in which Lata Mangeshkar rendered into living form the imaginative worlds of poets, composers, and filmmakers. The project is housed within the Department of Digital Humanities and aims to serve as a foundational resource for students, scholars, and general listeners who wish to engage seriously and systematically with her body of work.

The archive organises its holdings chronologically by decade, from the formative recordings of the 1940s through to the later-period work of the early 2000s, and thematically by emotional register, compositional lineage, and poetic tradition. Each song entry is accompanied by structured metadata, contextual annotation, and links to verified audio-visual sources. The project is committed to open access, non-commercial use, and transparent curatorial practice.

"Her voice was not merely heard; it was inhabited. For generations of listeners, Lata Mangeshkar's singing constituted the very texture of inner life."
— Project Editorial Statement, 2024

II

Aims and Objectives

The project proceeds from the conviction that Lata Mangeshkar's work constitutes a body of cultural heritage of the first order — one that has received sustained popular attention but comparatively limited rigorous scholarly treatment in English-language academic contexts. Chitralekha is designed to address this lacuna through the following aims and objectives.

01

Documentation

To create a structured, searchable, and continuously expanding catalogue of Lata Mangeshkar's recordings, prioritising depth of annotation over exhaustive enumeration, and ensuring that each entry meets consistent standards of bibliographic accuracy.

02

Contextualisation

To situate individual recordings within their broader cultural, historical, and compositional contexts — including the filmmaking traditions, poetic lineages, musical schools, and social circumstances from which they emerged.

03

Analysis

To publish original scholarly essays, data visualisations, and comparative studies that advance critical understanding of Lata Mangeshkar's vocal technique, stylistic range, and cultural significance within ethnomusicology, film studies, and South Asian cultural history.

04

Accessibility

To make the materials of this archive freely available to researchers, educators, students, and the interested public without restriction, in keeping with the principles of open access scholarship and the democratisation of humanistic inquiry.

05

Preservation

To support the long-term preservation of knowledge about a body of recordings that, while commercially available, remains inadequately documented in scholarly terms — particularly with regard to lesser-known regional-language recordings and early career work.

06

Community

To foster a scholarly and curatorial community around this archive, inviting contributions from researchers across disciplines and geographies, and modelling best practices in transparent, peer-reviewed digital curation.

III

Digital Humanities Approach

Chitralekha is designed in accordance with the principles and methodologies of digital humanities scholarship, understood as the application of computational, networked, and data-driven methods to questions arising within the humanities disciplines. The project draws on several traditions within this field, each of which shapes the architecture and practice of the archive in concrete ways.

Structured Data and Metadata Standards

Each song entry in the archive is encoded according to a consistent metadata schema that records film title, year of release, composer, lyricist, language, thematic classification, and archival annotation. This schema draws on the Dublin Core Metadata Initiative and adapts it for the specific requirements of South Asian film music documentation. Structured data enables both human navigation and machine-readable outputs for future computational analysis.

Corpus-Based Analysis

The archive's analytical dimension employs corpus-based methods to study patterns across Lata Mangeshkar's recorded output — including composer collaboration frequencies, language distribution over time, thematic clustering, and the structural features of raag-based compositions. These methods complement, rather than replace, close reading and interpretive scholarship, which remains central to the project's intellectual mission.

Linked Open Data and Interoperability

Where possible, the archive's metadata is aligned with existing linked open data initiatives, including Wikidata, MusicBrainz, and the National Film Archive of India's holdings. This commitment to interoperability reflects a broader aspiration to integrate Chitralekha with the emerging infrastructure of digital cultural heritage, ensuring that its records are discoverable and usable beyond the boundaries of the project's own interface.

Ethical Dimensions

The project is attentive to the ethical dimensions of digital archival work, including questions of intellectual property, cultural ownership, and the politics of representation. All audio-visual links connect to officially authorised or public-domain sources. The archive does not reproduce copyrighted material and is committed to acknowledging its own positionality and limitations as a scholarly enterprise.

Methods Employed

  • Metadata encoding (Dublin Core)
  • Corpus linguistics
  • Network analysis
  • Data visualisation
  • Linked open data
  • Digital bibliography
  • Oral history documentation

Disciplines Engaged

IV

Team Members

Chitralekha is a collaborative project sustained by an interdisciplinary team of researchers, archivists, and digital humanists. The project welcomes enquiries from prospective contributors and institutional partners.

A

Dr Aparna Krishnaswamy

Principal Investigator · Project Director

Associate Professor of Ethnomusicology and South Asian Studies. Her research focuses on the relationship between voice, gender, and national identity in mid-twentieth-century Indian film music. She is the author of The Feminine Timbre: Playback Singing and the Construction of Femininity in Hindi Cinema (2019).

Department of Musicology

R

Prof. Raghavendra Nair

Co-Investigator · Film History

Professor of Film and Cultural Studies with a specialisation in the Hindi film industry between 1940 and 1980. He has written extensively on the composer-singer relationship in the studio era and brings expertise in primary archival sources from the NFAI and Film Heritage Foundation collections.

School of Arts and Humanities

M

Dr Meenakshi Sundaram

Senior Research Associate · Urdu and Hindi Literary Traditions

A scholar of Urdu poetry and the ghazal tradition, Dr Sundaram leads the project's work on lyrical analysis and the relationship between film songs and the classical poetic canon. She holds a doctorate from Jawaharlal Nehru University and has published on Sahir Ludhianvi, Shailendra, and Majrooh Sultanpuri.

Centre for Language and Literature

P

Priya Venkataraman

Digital Humanities Lead · Metadata Architecture

A specialist in digital archiving and cultural heritage informatics, Priya is responsible for the archive's technical infrastructure, metadata schema design, and data visualisation components. She holds an MSc in Digital Humanities and has previously worked with the British Library's South Asia collections.

Digital Humanities Laboratory

S

Siddharth Oberoi

Research Assistant · Discography and Source Verification

A doctoral candidate in cultural history whose dissertation examines the commercial recording industry in colonial and post-independence India. Siddharth leads the project's source-verification work, cross-referencing entries against primary discographic records and institutional filmography databases.

Department of History

N

Dr Nalini Chakraborty

Associate Researcher · Bengali and Regional Repertoire

A specialist in Bengali cultural history and the Rabindra Sangeet tradition, Dr Chakraborty oversees documentation of Lata Mangeshkar's non-Hindi recordings, with particular attention to her Bengali, Marathi, and Gujarati language output — areas that remain significantly under-documented in existing scholarship.

Department of Modern Languages

Advisory Board

The project benefits from the guidance of an international advisory board comprising scholars in ethnomusicology, South Asian studies, digital humanities, and archival science. Members are affiliated with the Indira Gandhi National Centre for the Arts, the Sangeet Natak Akademi, the School of Oriental and African Studies (University of London), and the University of California, Berkeley.

The project also acknowledges the invaluable contributions of community archivists, dedicated listeners, and independent researchers whose decades of careful documentation of Lata Mangeshkar's recordings have provided an indispensable foundation for this scholarly endeavour.
