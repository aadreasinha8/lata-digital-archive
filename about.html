About the Project

Chitralekha: Lata Mangeshkar Digital Archive is an open-access digital humanities project dedicated to the systematic documentation, contextualisation, and scholarly analysis of the musical legacy of Lata Mangeshkar (28 September 1929 â€“ 6 February 2022). The archive takes its name from the Sanskrit word chitralekha â€” "she who draws pictures" â€” invoking the manner in which Lata's voice rendered visible the interior emotional landscapes of generations of listeners.

The project proceeds from the conviction that Lata Mangeshkar's body of work constitutes one of the most significant cultural archives of twentieth-century South Asia. Over a career spanning more than seven decades, she recorded in excess of 30,000 songs across thirty-six languages and dialects, collaborating with virtually every major composer, lyricist, and filmmaker of the Hindi film industry. Despite the scale and cultural centrality of this output, it has received comparatively limited treatment within formal academic scholarship.

This archive seeks to address that lacuna. By assembling structured, cross-referenced metadata for a representative corpus of songs, and by pairing each entry with contextual notes grounded in musicological, historical, and cultural analysis, Chitralekha aims to provide a resource that is simultaneously useful to researchers, accessible to students, and legible to engaged non-specialist listeners.

The project is conceived as a living archive: an evolving, expandable resource that will grow through sustained curatorial effort, peer contribution, and iterative editorial review. It is hosted as a freely accessible, non-commercial resource in the spirit of open scholarship, and welcomes collaborative engagement from scholars across the disciplines of musicology, film studies, cultural history, South Asian studies, and the digital humanities.

"To understand Lata Mangeshkar's voice is to understand something essential about the emotional history of modern India."

Aims and Objectives

The project is guided by a set of clearly articulated aims, each reflecting a specific gap in the existing landscape of scholarship on Indian film music and on Lata Mangeshkar's work in particular. These aims are operationalised through the five principal sections of the archive.

01

Documentation & Preservation

To assemble a rigorously sourced, cross-referenced corpus of song metadata â€” including title, film, year, composer, lyricist, language, and recording context â€” and to make this corpus freely available in structured, citable form.

02

Contextualisation

To situate each archival entry within its historical, cinematic, and compositional context, drawing on scholarly literature, contemporary criticism, and primary source materials held by institutions including the National Film Archive of India and the Film Heritage Foundation.

03

Thematic Analysis

To identify and map recurring themes, aesthetic patterns, and formal structures across the corpus â€” including the deployment of specific ragas, the treatment of viraha and bhakti, and the evolution of Lata's vocal technique across decades.

04

Data-Driven Inquiry

To employ computational methods â€” including corpus analysis, network visualisation of composerâ€“lyricist collaborations, and acoustic analysis of recordings â€” to generate new scholarly insights not readily available through traditional close-reading approaches alone.

05

Access & Pedagogy

To make high-quality scholarship on Hindi film music accessible to students, educators, and interested general readers, including through the provision of curated listening paths, annotated thematic collections, and open access to all analytical content.

06

Community & Collaboration

To foster a community of scholars and practitioners engaged with the study of Indian film music, providing a platform for peer contribution, editorial review, and ongoing dialogue between academic and non-academic forms of expertise.

Digital Humanities Approach

Chitralekha is situated within the interdisciplinary field of digital humanities, understood here as the application of computational tools and methods to humanistic questions, and conversely, the application of humanistic modes of inquiry â€” critical, interpretive, contextual â€” to digital forms and practices.

The archive takes a deliberately pluralist methodological stance. Rather than privileging any single approach, it draws on the complementary strengths of close reading, corpus linguistics, musicological analysis, cultural history, and data visualisation. This pluralism reflects the complexity of the object of study: a body of work that is at once musicologically intricate, historically embedded, culturally multivalent, and numerically vast.

The choice to build a structured digital archive rather than to produce a conventional monograph reflects a set of epistemological commitments: that knowledge about Lata Mangeshkar's work is necessarily distributed, emergent, and provisional; that its production requires collaborative effort across multiple forms of expertise; and that its dissemination should be as wide and as unencumbered as possible.

All data generated by this project is made available under open licences. Computational notebooks, raw datasets, and methodological documentation are archived in a public repository, in accordance with the principles of open scholarship and reproducible research.
